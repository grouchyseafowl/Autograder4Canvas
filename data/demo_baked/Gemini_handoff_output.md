Assignment Analysis: Week 6 Discussion: Intersectionality in Practice

Course: Ethnic Studies (11), Period 3
Submissions: 32

What Your Students Said

Executive Summary: Your students overwhelmingly grasped the core of Crenshaw’s framework. Most moved beyond the definition to actively apply the theory to their own lives, demonstrating a high degree of knowledge production. Rather than discussing intersectionality as abstract theory, they utilized it to make sense of generational trauma, medical bias, and school discipline.

Biggest Win: The profound translation of legal theory into familial lineage. A massive portion of your class mapped Crenshaw’s 1989 legal critique onto the bodies and experiences of their mothers, grandmothers, and siblings. They proved that they don't just understand the concept—they have been living it.

Emergent Themes

1. Matriarchal Lineages as Lived Theory
Many students used the experiences of their mothers and grandmothers as primary texts to validate Crenshaw's framework.

Yolanda Fuentes wrote of her abuela: "It's not just that she's old, or just that she speaks Spanish first, or just that she cleaned houses her whole life. It's all of those things at the same time, and they all weigh different depending on where she's standing."

Priya Venkataraman observed how her mother's identities "compound each other in a specific way" during parent-teacher conferences, noting that fixing just sexism still leaves accent bias.

Aaliyah Robinson shared her great-aunt's experience in the Jim Crow South and Chicago: "It's not that you walk through one door called race and another called gender. It's that every intersection has its own door..."

Ingrid Vasquez analyzed her mother's vulnerability in a textile factory: "Any one of those things she might have found a way around. All of them together meant there was no way out."

2. The Limits of an "American" Framework
Several students from immigrant families engaged critically with the text, pointing out a blind spot in Crenshaw's strictly American racial categorization and pushing the theory to be more global.

Amara Diallo: "It was built on American categories and my grandmother's experience doesn't map cleanly onto American racial hierarchies. There's a whole layer about colonialism and francophone identity..."

Linh Tran: Analyzed the ethnic minority (Hmong) vs. majority (Kinh) dynamics in Vietnam, noting: "I wish the reading included more examples from outside America... the framework should be big enough to hold them."

Maria Ndiaye: "I think the reading could be stronger if it include voices from outside America because this experience it is everywhere not just here."

3. "The Default" in Systems (Healthcare and Education)
Students keenly recognized that systems are built for specific "default" identities, leaving those with intersecting marginalizations at risk.

Sofia Esparza: Applied this to her cousin in nursing: "Intersectionality isn't just a framework for understanding oppression, it's a framework for understanding how things go wrong in systems that were built with a specific kind of person in mind as the default."

Rashida Thompson: Applied it to school discipline: "The students who get suspended most aren't just Black students — it's specifically Black girls who get coded as 'aggressive'..."

Elijah Summers: Applied it to neighborhood policing: "Systems get built with a certain kind of person in mind and everyone else has to navigate around that."

4. Theory for Analysis vs. Action
A distinct intellectual curiosity emerged around what to do after identifying an intersection.

Jasmine Holloway: "I do wonder whether intersectionality is mostly useful for analysis or whether it also tells you what to do... I think the reading was clearer on the diagnosis than on the treatment."

Sierra Nakamura: "Can you use it to decide what to do, or mostly just to describe what's happening?"

Tensions & Contradictions

(Explicitly preserved for pedagogical exploration)

Tension 1: Class Erasure vs. Class Integration

Jake Novak strongly feels the framework erased his lived reality. He argues that intersectionality has a "blind spot for class when its separated from race," asking: "Where does my dad's privilege come in? He cant pay the electric bill half the time."

Destiny Williams, however, sees class perfectly integrated into the framework through the lens of structural history (redlining), arguing: "my grandmother was Black AND poor AND a woman AND living in a neighborhood the government decided wasnt worth investing in." * Why this is generative: Jake is asking a vital question about white poverty in anti-racist discourse. Destiny is modeling how economic deprivation is literally mapped into geography. Bringing these two views into conversation about how capitalism operates alongside white supremacy would be powerful.

Tension 2: Political Urgency vs. The Demand for "Civility"

Destiny Williams embraces righteous anger as a valid academic register: "This reading made me furious and I mean that in a good way... Im tired of pretending we can discuss it calmly like it doesnt affect real people right now."

Aiden Brooks explicitly requests the opposite: "I feel like when people start getting angry or raising their voices it actually makes it harder to have a productive conversation... We should focus on understanding each others perspectives."

Why this is generative: This perfectly surfaces the concept of tone policing. Destiny points out that forced calmness is a privilege when discussing violence. Aiden’s discomfort is a chance to discuss why academic spaces traditionally value detached neutrality over lived urgency.

Surprises

(Outliers and unexpected connections)

Camille Osei: Synthesized the reading with outside media (the Maintenance Phase podcast) to critique the BMI as a medical tool built exclusively on "the white European male body." Her conclusion is stunning: "The BMI is a framework built for one person and extended to everyone. So was the law Crenshaw was critiquing."

Jordan Espinoza: Leveraged their neurodivergent writing style as meta-commentary on the theory itself: "I have a lot more to say but my thoughts aren't organized in the way an essay is supposed to be organized... the intersections are real even when the essay isn't perfect."

Imani Drayton: Masterfully connected 1989 legal theory to the unspoken "texture" of modern school hallways. "It's not always in a way you can point to and say that's the thing. It's more like a texture. The expectations are different. The patience is different."

Your Focus Areas

1. Student Engagement with Lived Experience
This was exceptionally high. Rather than relying on Crenshaw's examples, students actively mapped the theory onto:

Undocumented labor (Ingrid Vasquez, Yolanda Fuentes)

Disability, Deaf culture, and ADHD (Sierra Nakamura, DeShawn Mercer, Jordan Espinoza)

The medical field (Sofia Esparza, Camille Osei)

2. Colorblind Ideology
This ideology surfaced distinctly in two places. Connor Walsh explicitly stated, "I just try to treat everyone the same... I dont really see the point of focusing so much on categories and labels." Additionally, Aiden Brooks's plea for "respectful" and non-emotional dialogue is a softer manifestation of colorblind ideology—it seeks to neutralize the very real, structural violence the framework is designed to highlight.

3. AAVE and Multilingual Voices
Your students are producing brilliant academic knowledge through their authentic registers.

Imani Drayton uses AAVE ("I been knowing") not as slang, but as an epistemological stance—she possessed this knowledge long before academia gave it a name.

Maria Ndiaye and Linh Tran use multilingual syntactic structures that reflect their immigrant experiences, proving that standard English isn't required to deliver high-level theoretical critiques (like their points on globalizing the framework).

What Your Students Need You to See (Concern Flags)

Connor Walsh * Flag: Colorblind/post-racial claims dismissing structural inequality. ("I just see a person. I think if more people had that attitude we wouldnt need frameworks like intersectionality")

Suggested Response: Acknowledge his intention (interpersonal kindness) but challenge him to separate interpersonal treatment from systemic reality. Ask: "If a law or a hospital policy discriminates against a specific group, does treating someone nicely in the hallway fix the policy?"

Aiden Brooks

Flag: Tone policing that silences others' emotional responses. ("I think we should be able to have these conversations without getting so emotional about it.")

Suggested Response: Introduce the concept of "tone policing" to the class next week. Frame it as a question of privilege: Who has the luxury of discussing systemic oppression "calmly"? Validate that discomfort in the classroom is often a sign that real learning is happening.
