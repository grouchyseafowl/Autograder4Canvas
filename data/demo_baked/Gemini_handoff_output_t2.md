Assignment Analysis: Week 6 Discussion: Intersectionality in Practice

What Your Students Said

Overall, your students demonstrated an incredibly high level of engagement with a dense, graduate-level theoretical framework. Most bypassed abstract summary and immediately moved to apply the concept to their lives. The emotional register of the class is passionate, urgent, and deeply reflective.

Biggest Win: Students aren't just memorizing Crenshaw's definition; they are testing its limits. Multiple students explicitly asked how a framework built around American civil rights law applies to global, refugee, and colonial contexts. They are doing the work of true scholars—using theory as a lens, finding its gaps, and stretching it to fit their realities.

Emergent Themes

Theme 1: Testing the American Framework Globally
Several multilingual and immigrant students are asking brilliant questions about how intersectionality translates outside the US racial binary.

Maria Ndiaye: "Intersectionality for me it is not theory it is just what my family live through and I think the reading could be stronger if it include voices from outside America"

Amara Diallo: Pointed out that her grandmother in France was "too African" and in Guinea was "too French." She noted: "There's a whole layer about colonialism and francophone identity that intersectionality doesn't fully account for."

Priya Venkataraman: "I wonder whether the framework travels to my mom's situation exactly the same way or if there's something slightly different about South Asian immigrant women."

Linh Tran: Pointed out how the "refugee" identity operates differently than other racial categories and how the framework needs to hold the "ethnic minority group Hmong" context.

Theme 2: Systemic Invisibility and Real-World Consequences
Students vividly connected theoretical invisibility to material consequences, particularly in healthcare and labor.

Sofia Esparza: Analyzed how doctors ignore her cousin Valentina (a heavier, young, Latina CNA without a degree): "It's all of it arriving at once and someone decides in a few seconds whether to take her seriously... A patient doesn't get pain medication. A condition gets missed."

Ingrid Vasquez: Shared her mother's experience as an undocumented, pregnant textile worker: "Any one of those things she might have found a way around. All of them together meant there was no way out."

Yolanda Fuentes: Wrote about her abuela Esperanza: "It's not just that she's old, or just that she speaks Spanish first, or just that she cleaned houses her whole life. It's all of those things at the same time, and they all weigh different depending on where she's standing."

Theme 3: School Discipline and the "Default" Student
This theme is the perfect bridge to your upcoming unit on structural racism and policing.

Rashida Thompson: "The students who get suspended most aren't just Black students — it's specifically Black girls who get coded as 'aggressive' or 'threatening' in ways that white girls doing the same behavior never are."

DeShawn Mercer: Analyzed his 9-year-old Black, ADHD brother Malik getting discipline referrals while white kids get redirected: "And it's about being Black, because Black boys get read as a threat before anyone else."

Theme 4: Analysis vs. Action
A few students understand the theory but are hungry for the "so what now?"

Jasmine Holloway: "I do wonder whether intersectionality is mostly useful for analysis or whether it also tells you what to do... I think the reading was clearer on the diagnosis than on the treatment."

Sierra Nakamura: "Is intersectionality a framework for analysis or also for action?"

Tensions & Contradictions

Race vs. Class (The "Privilege" Blindspot)

Jake Novak surfaced a crucial and productive tension. He felt erased by the framework as a poor white student: "When the reading says that privilege operates along axes of race and gender I want to ask where does my dad's privilege come in? He cant pay the electric bill half the time."

Pedagogical Value: This is a perfect opportunity to validate Jake's reality (class struggle) while pulling in Kevin Osei's point that "working class" is an intersectional axis. Jake is actually making an intersectional argument without realizing it—that whiteness separated from class wealth doesn't protect you from poverty.

Surprises

Camille Osei: Produced an incredible outlier by applying Crenshaw's legal critique to the BMI medical scale via a podcast she listens to outside of class. "The BMI was developed in the 1800s using only white European men as the data set... The tool itself encodes the assumption that the white European male body is the standard." This is phenomenal synthesis.

Aaliyah Robinson: Created a beautiful metaphor about Jim Crow "doors" based on her great-aunt Lorraine, brilliantly threading in colorism ("dark-skinned even among other Black folks") and regionalism ("sorted by being Southern").

Your Focus Areas

1. Student engagement with lived experience: Stunningly high. Students like Destiny Williams are vibrating with urgent connection ("Intersectionality isnt just an academic concept. Its the story of my family").

2. Colorblind ideology: This emerged explicitly in one submission (Connor Walsh), giving you a clear example of the exact ideology you want to dismantle next week.

3. AAVE and multilingual voices: Your students are proving that standard academic English is not a prerequisite for rigorous academic thought.

Imani Drayton's use of AAVE is theoretically profound: "I been knowing that being Black and being a girl is its own specific thing... It's like a whole different channel that comes with its own static."

Jordan Espinoza advocated for their neurodivergent formatting perfectly: "the intersections are real even when the essay isn't perfect."

What Your Students Need You to See (Concern Flags)

Connor Walsh (Colorblind Claim): "I dont really see the point of focusing so much on categories and labels because I feel like that just divides people more. When I meet someone I dont think about their race or gender I just see a person."

Suggested Response: Don't shame him, but challenge the premise. Ask: "If a system (like the 1989 courts Crenshaw studied) was built to disadvantage a certain group, how does pretending not to see the group help fix the system?"

Aiden Brooks (Tone Policing): "I feel like when people start getting angry or raising their voices it actually makes it harder to have a productive conversation... We should focus on understanding each others perspectives instead of trying to win arguments."

Suggested Response: Address this in the whole-class space (without naming Aiden). Affirm that anger about injustice is an appropriate and historically vital academic response, not a disruption to learning.

Brittany Okafor (Essentializing Language): "the Mexican families are always so close... And the Black families on my street they have this amazing resilience..."

Suggested Response: Gently help her see that even "positive" stereotypes flatten people. Ask: "What happens to a Black person who is exhausted and doesn't want to be resilient? Are they allowed to just be tired?"
