## Section 1: Methodology Audit

> This section gates everything that follows. No comparison finding or code change should be accepted without first checking whether it's conditioned on a methodology artifact.

### 1.1 Situating the Prior Evaluation

The prior pipeline evaluation (`docs/pipeline_evaluation.md`, 2026-03-21) was produced under specific conditions that shape its findings:

- **Full session context**: The evaluator had been working in the codebase all day and had deep familiarity with the pipeline architecture
- **No test apparatus examination**: The evaluator did not read `scripts/assemble_demo_corpus.py` or investigate how the 29 submissions were constructed
- **Detection-frame language**: The evaluation uses language like "off-topic," "critical gap," and "hallucinated theme assignment" — framing from the detection paradigm, not the engagement reframe
- **Incomplete synthesis**: Only 3 of 9 synthesis sections populated due to JSON parse errors in the 8B model output. Several findings may be artifacts of this incomplete run.

This doesn't make the evaluation wrong — it makes it *situated*. (#FEMINIST_TECHNOSCIENCE: all knowledge is produced from a position. The evaluation's position was technically informed but methodologically unexamined.) We name its conditions so we can use it accurately, not so we can dismiss it.

### 1.3 Classification of Prior Evaluation Findings

Filtering every finding from `docs/pipeline_evaluation.md` through the methodology audit:

#### ✅ Valid Regardless of Methodology

These findings hold even knowing the test data was poorly constructed:

1. **Feedback-concern contradiction** (Connor Walsh) — The pipeline's concern detector correctly flagged Connor's colorblind framing (0.85 confidence), but the feedback drafter — running later without access to concern flags — produced feedback that validated that same framing. This is a real broken mycorrhizal link: Stage 5 (concerns) doesn't share data with Stage 8 (feedback). Found on hand-crafted data.

2. **Concern detection accuracy on hand-crafted students** — The pipeline correctly flagged:
   - Brittany Okafor: essentializing language (0.8 confidence)
   - Connor Walsh: colorblind claim (0.85 confidence)
   - Aiden Brooks: tone policing (0.85 confidence)
   And correctly did NOT flag:
   - Destiny Williams: righteous anger treated as appropriate engagement
   - Jake Novak: premise challenge treated as legitimate intellectual work
   - Maria Ndiaye: ESL patterns not treated as concern

3. **ESL treatment of Maria Ndiaye** — Pipeline coded her accurately: "connecting theory to family history," "intersectionality in multiple contexts," emotional register "personal." Her transnational perspective (grandmother in Dakar, mother's experience as immigrant) was captured. (#COMMUNITY_CULTURAL_WEALTH: her family knowledge is an analytical asset, and the pipeline recognized it.)

4. **Inter-stage data flow breaks** — Architectural issue independent of test data. Quick Analysis computes clusters, embeddings, keyword hits, and sentiment — but coding prompts receive only vader_compound, keyword_hits, and cluster_id (a number without context). Concern flags don't reach the feedback drafter's content decisions. Theme generation doesn't receive cluster metadata.

5. **Alex Hernandez smoking gun missed** — The pipeline coded S003's HTML-tagged submission (`<h2>`, `<p>`, `<h3>`) as legitimate content without flagging the paste artifacts. The coding stage has no smoking-gun detection — that's the AIC's job, and the two systems don't share data during pipeline runs.

6. **Jordan Kim burnout signal missed** — The pipeline coded S002's truncated submission ("Idk I had more to say but its late and") without noting it as incomplete/truncated.

#### ⚠️ Needs Reinterpretation

These findings are real but their significance changes with methodology context:

7. **Coding hallucination** (David Park S007 coded with "intersectionality in practice" at 0.8) — The hallucination is real: the 8B model attributed a concept the student never mentioned. But the test setup provoked it: the 8B was given a driving essay in an intersectionality assignment context. The model tried to connect the two and fabricated a link. **Still worth fixing** (the hallucination guard is good engineering), but this is not evidence that the 8B model can't code well-constructed submissions. On the 10 hand-crafted students, coding was accurate.

8. **Individual pair surfacing** (Ethan Liu / Nadia Petrov) — Their "verbatim match" is a data construction artifact: both derived from `daigt_00011`. This pair should NOT be used to validate the individual surfacing feature. We need a real pair to test against.

9. **Tyler Nguyen and Jaylen Carter not flagged as concerns** — Both are AI-generated (Ollama llama3.1:8b), marked as `sustained_cheat` in the corpus. The pipeline coded them cleanly with no concern flags. Under the engagement reframe, this is actually correct behavior in part: the pipeline should surface their *disengagement* (zero personal connection, generic academic register), not try to detect AI. But it didn't surface the disengagement either — both got clean emotional register "analytical" without noting the absence of personal investment. (#ETHNIC_STUDIES: a student phoning it in on an Ethnic Studies assignment shows up as absence across every engagement dimension — the system should catch that.)

#### ❌ Likely Artifacts of Test Data Construction or Incomplete Run

10. **"19 of 29 students submitted wrong assignment"** — Not a pipeline gap discovery. Opus correctly read our poorly-constructed test data. The DAIGT essays were never actually about intersectionality. The pipeline can't be blamed for not detecting a mismatch that exists in our test data construction, not in real student behavior.

11. **Theme quality — 30 themes with massive redundancy** — Multiple potential artifact sources compound here:
    - **Mixed-topic corpus**: Driving themes and intersectionality themes jammed together produce incoherent output. On a coherent corpus, theme quality would be assessed differently.
    - **Pipeline crash / incomplete deduplication**: The theme generator uses a 3-pass synthesis approach for 8B reliability. If the pipeline crashed or timed out mid-run, deduplication passes may not have completed. The 5+ near-identical driving themes ("risks and consequences of distracted driving," "distracted driving risks," "concern for safety," "concern about public safety," "public safety concerns") could be artifacts of incomplete multi-pass synthesis rather than a fundamental deduplication gap. **Verified**: Theme generation produced 18 LLM-generated themes (0.7-0.9 confidence) + 12 tag-frequency fallbacks (0.3 confidence, labeled "Auto-grouped from coding tag ... LLM timed out" — see `theme_generator.py:_fallback_theme_set_from_records()` line 66-93). So theme generation partially completed: 18 groups got LLM synthesis, 12 groups timed out and fell back to tag aggregation. The redundancy is a **compound artifact**: mixed topics + partial completion + no deduplication pass across the boundary between LLM-generated and fallback themes.
    - **Can't determine root cause without**: (a) checking whether all theme generation passes completed, and (b) running on a coherent single-topic corpus.

12. **12 fallback themes being intersectionality-related** — The LLM timed out on the intersectionality submissions (the pedagogically important ones), producing tag-frequency fallbacks with 0.3 confidence and no descriptions. This may reflect genuine 8B difficulty with complex material, or may be a cascading effect of the timeout/crash that produced the theme redundancy above. Same verification needed: did the theme generation stage fully complete, or did it crash/timeout partway through?

13. **Synthesis sections incomplete** (3/9) — JSON parse errors in 8B output. This was a runtime failure, not a design gap. The biology pipeline later crashed with the same class of error (numeric value in sections dict). A validator fix now exists in the working tree (`models.py` lines 322-340).

### 1.4 New Issues Surfaced by This Audit

Issues the prior evaluation missed because it wasn't looking through these lenses:

14. **Disengagement detection gap** — Tyler Nguyen and Jaylen Carter (AI-generated, zero personal engagement) got clean codings with no signal that the teacher should check in. Under the engagement reframe, these students should show up as "limited engagement" across personal connection, personal investment, and real-time thinking dimensions. The pipeline doesn't compute engagement dimensions — that's the AIC's job, and the two systems are separate.

15. **Both/and tension in practice** — The pipeline currently produces coding records that could serve either frame (detection or engagement) but serve neither well. The concern detector correctly flags pedagogical concerns (essentializing, colorblindness, tone policing) but doesn't flag engagement absence. The coding stage produces theme tags and emotional register but doesn't assess engagement depth. Neither system crosses the bridge between the two.

16. **Similarity analysis grain** — Document-level cosine similarity at 0.90 threshold catches near-verbatim matches. But the more interesting signal for AI-assisted work is **moderate similarity with different wording** (0.6-0.8 range) — students who asked the same AI the same prompt get semantically similar but lexically different output. This is a paragraph-level or sentence-cluster analysis problem, not a document-level one. The current pairwise similarity operates at document level only. Future work should explore finer-grained analysis, but this is beyond the current plan's scope.

### Summary: Where 8B Performs Well vs Where It Doesn't

**8B performs well on**:
- Content coding of genuine student work (Maria, Destiny, Jake, Connor — all accurately coded)
- Concern detection for specific pedagogical patterns (essentializing, colorblindness, tone policing)
- NOT flagging righteous anger or legitimate critique

**8B does NOT perform well on**:
- Detecting engagement ABSENCE (Tyler, Jaylen coded as "engaging" despite zero personal investment)
- Characterizing rhetorical MOVES (Opus names "uses respect language to dismiss structural analysis"; 8B flags "colorblind claim" without naming the move)
- Emotional register precision (Brittany coded as "passionate" when "confused" is more useful; Aiden coded as "analytical" when "defensive" would be more accurate)
- Self-consistency between theme tags and concerns (Brittany: "celebrating cultural diversity" + "essentializing language" in the same record)

**What's genuinely beyond 8B**:
- Evaluative judgment about intellectual quality ("genuine framework limitation" vs just "framework critique")
- Naming the pedagogical significance of a student's move
- Connecting individual submissions to broader scholarly conversations

**What could be approximated through architecture**:
- Engagement absence → wire AIC engagement dimensions into pipeline records
- Self-consistency → post-validation that theme tags don't contradict concerns
- Incomplete submission detection → non-LLM check for truncation patterns

## Section 4: Root Cause Analysis

### Gap 1: Feedback-Concern Contradiction
- **Type**: Broken mycorrhizal link
- **Location**: `engine.py` Stage 5 (concerns) → Stage 8 (feedback) — no data flow
- **Root cause**: `feedback_drafter.py:_build_concern_context()` (line 110-132) builds tone guidance only ("Write with extra warmth") without any content awareness. The feedback LLM doesn't know WHAT the concern is about, so it may validate the flagged behavior.
- **Fix**: Pass concern content summaries to feedback drafter
- **Methodology status**: ✅ Valid — found on hand-crafted data

### Gap 2: Coding Hallucination
- **Type**: 8B model limitation + missing guard
- **Location**: `submission_coder.py` — no post-validation of `concepts_applied`
- **Root cause**: The 8B model sees the assignment prompt and infers concepts that "should" be in the submission, even when they're not. More severe with off-topic submissions (test data artifact) but can occur with on-topic submissions too (attributing concepts mentioned in the prompt but not by the student).
- **Fix**: Post-validate concepts against submission text
- **Methodology status**: ⚠️ Provoked by test data but still worth fixing

### Gap 3: Assignment Connection (vocabulary overlap)
- **Type**: Missing non-LLM capability
- **Location**: Would go in `quick_analyzer.py`
- **Root cause**: The pipeline has no mechanism to assess whether a submission's vocabulary connects to the assignment description. In real deployment, this catches wrong-portal submissions and helps teachers see which students are connecting to the assigned material.
- **Fix**: TF-IDF cosine similarity between assignment description and each submission
- **Methodology status**: ❌ Cannot validate against current corpus (the artifact provoked the finding, but the capability is real)
- **Equity risk**: Vocabulary overlap can't assess experiential engagement (#COMMUNITY_CULTURAL_WEALTH). Must frame as informational, never evaluative.

### Gap 4: Individual Pair Surfacing
- **Type**: Missing capability (deliberately excluded)
- **Location**: `quick_analyzer.py:_pairwise_similarity()` — by design only returns class-level stats
- **Root cause**: Design decision to avoid surfacing individual pairs due to equity risk. The handoff document and reframe spec both specify surfacing at >= 0.90 cosine similarity only, with equity framing.
- **Fix**: Add individual pair identification at extreme threshold
- **Methodology status**: ⚠️ Ethan/Nadia match is a DAIGT artifact — need a real pair to validate
- **Design consideration**: Document-level 0.90 cosine catches near-verbatim matches. For AI-assisted work (similar answers, different wording), paragraph-level or sentence-cluster analysis at lower thresholds would be more informative. This is future work.

### Gap 5: Inter-Stage Data Flow
- **Type**: Architectural — broken mycorrhizal links
- **Location**: `engine.py` orchestration
- **Root cause**: Pipeline stages run sequentially but don't share outputs:
  - Quick Analysis → coding: only vader_compound, keyword_hits, cluster_id (a number). Missing: cluster composition, top terms, pairwise stats, assignment connection
  - Concerns → feedback: only existence (boolean), not content
  - Clusters → theme generation: themes generated per-cluster but without cluster metadata
- **Fix**: Wire Quick Analysis outputs into downstream stages; pass concern content to feedback
- **Methodology status**: ✅ Valid — architectural issue independent of test data

### Gap 6: Engagement Absence Detection (NEW — not in prior evaluation)
- **Type**: Missing capability
- **Root cause**: Tyler Nguyen and Jaylen Carter (AI-generated, zero personal engagement) got clean codings because the pipeline codes CONTENT, not ENGAGEMENT. The AIC computes engagement dimensions, but these don't flow into the Insights pipeline.
- **Fix**: Either wire AIC engagement signals into pipeline records, or add engagement dimension assessment to the coding stage. The former is simpler; the latter requires the 8B to do something it may not do well.
- **Methodology status**: ✅ Valid — engagement absence is real on both AI-generated and disengaged human submissions

### Gap 7: Theme Tag / Concern Self-Contradiction (NEW — not in prior evaluation)
- **Type**: Internal consistency failure
- **Root cause**: Brittany Okafor's record contains both "celebrating cultural diversity" (theme tag) and "essentializing language" (concern). Aiden Brooks's record contains "acknowledging emotional complexity" (theme tag) and "tone policing" (concern). The coding and concern stages run independently and can contradict.
- **Fix**: Post-validation: if a concern is flagged, check whether any theme tags validate the flagged behavior. If so, adjust or annotate the theme tag.
- **Methodology status**: ✅ Valid — found on hand-crafted data

### Gap 8: Incomplete Submission Detection (NEW — not in prior evaluation)
- **Type**: Missing non-LLM capability
- **Root cause**: Jordan Kim's submission cuts off mid-sentence. The pipeline treats it as complete. A simple text heuristic (last sentence ends without punctuation, word count well below class median) could flag truncated submissions.
- **Fix**: Add truncation check in `quick_analyzer.py` or `engine.py`
- **Methodology status**: ✅ Valid

## Design Tensions to Surface

These are conversations, not conclusions. The professor has the pedagogical expertise; the technical team has the architectural knowledge. Neither has the full picture alone.

### Tension 1: Vocabulary Overlap vs. Experiential Engagement

TF-IDF assignment connection catches wrong-portal submissions but may undercount students who engage through lived experience. Maria Ndiaye's grandmother in Dakar IS intersectionality — but she may score lower on vocabulary overlap because her entry point is experiential, not academic.

**The both/and**: Vocabulary overlap is one signal among many, not a gate. The observation text must genuinely hold open: "This student may be approaching the material through personal experience or a different entry point." But we also need to acknowledge: if 19 of 29 students have < 10% vocabulary overlap, the teacher DOES need to know that, and "approaching differently" is not the most likely explanation for 19 students simultaneously.

### Tension 2: Similarity Analysis Grain

Document-level cosine at 0.90 catches near-verbatim copying. But the emerging pattern with AI-assisted work is moderate similarity with different wording — students who asked the same AI the same prompt get semantically similar but lexically varied output. This is a paragraph-level or sentence-cluster problem. Figures like 0.90 document-level similarity might make more sense at paragraph level. And cluster-level analysis of similar sentence structures across submissions could catch the pattern where multiple students get AI-generated answers that are thematically identical but syntactically varied.

**For now**: Implement document-level 0.90 pair surfacing as specified. Document this grain limitation as future architectural work.

### Tension 3: Concern-Aware Feedback vs. Student Autonomy

Steering feedback away from validating colorblindness is good pedagogy. But how far should the system go in shaping what a teacher says to a student? The system provides drafts, never final text — the teacher decides. But the drafting LLM IS making pedagogical choices: it's deciding what to validate and what to redirect. This is inherently a values-laden process, and the system should be transparent about whose values are encoded. (#FEMINIST_TECHNOSCIENCE)

**For now**: Pass concern content to feedback drafter with clear guidance. The teacher reviews and edits all drafts before sending.
